Flexible Hybrid Electronics Research at Tapecon

During my internship at Tapecon Inc., I worked at the forefront of Flexible Hybrid Electronics (FHE) development, contributing to the company’s cutting-edge smart label and sensor technologies. Located in the historic Larkin building in Buffalo, NY, Tapecon specializes in advanced manufacturing of flexible circuits, adhesives, and printed electronics.

+

During my first year at Tapecon, I worked to develop flexible circuit demonstrators intended to show off the capabilities of Tapecon's new FHE production line. These demonstrators were showcased at NextFlex Innovation Days in California and at a sister conference in Berlin, highlighting Tapecon's advancements in flexible hybrid printed electronics on an international stage.

I moved on from demonstrators and began working directly on preparing the FHE line for production. One of my projects centered around improving the bonding and placement processes critical to FHE reliability. I designed and executed a series of controlled experiments to optimize bonding conditions for circuit components to PET and paper substrates. This involved tuning bonding conditions to enhance adhesion and yield while reducing delamination during production. These refinements were integrated directly into the production line, increasing throughput and reducing defect rates.

I also played a lead role in calibrating the Universal Pick and Place (PnP) Fiducial Acquisition system. The existing setup misaligned components on the flexible substrates, causing functional errors in microcontroller performance. I identified that fiducials were missing in the acquisition logic and implemented a revised vision scan protocol with global correction settings. After deployment, placement precision improved dramatically, ensuring repeatable accuracy across batches.

In addition to hands-on process engineering, I contributed to technical documentation, data tracking, and troubleshooting across the FHE R&D line. I also coordinated with some of Tapecon's customers, using their feedback to develop the process for manufacturing certain products.

Digital Twin Campus Prototype

This award-winning project, recognized with the Russell L. Agrusa Student Innovation Award, focused on the development of a fully operational Digital Twin framework that can be applied to a manufacturing process. Our prototype applied this framework to a 3D printer. The system integrates physical manufacturing equipment with a cyber-physical interface for real-time monitoring and remote control. By 2023 I became the research team lead. Beforehand, from June to August 2022, I led the hardware development team, designing and implementing the embedded systems infrastructure using ESP32 microcontrollers, Arduino platforms, and WebSocket protocols to enable seamless data transmission and cloud-based control. I traveled with Matthew Rubino to Boston to present our project to the 2022 Sensors and Systems Conference, leading to the publication of our work in the ACM Digital Library.

+

Developed as part of a cross-functional team of Electrical Engineers and Computer Engineers, the prototype used embedded sensors, IoT frameworks, and 3D digital modeling tools to reflect the live state of physical infrastructure. The system was designed for scalability, allowing future integration with smart city initiatives. The project received institutional recognition for its technical depth, innovation, and potential long-term societal benefit.

Our prototype connected a Creality Ender-3 Pro 3D printer with a React-based UI to demonstrate a scalable model for Industry 4.0. My contributions included architecting the hardware-software communication backbone that allowed the system to read real-time sensor data (vibration, pressure, camera streams) and issue G-code commands back to the printer via the cloud. I implemented robust serial and WebSocket communication between ESP32 microcontrollers, an Arduino Mega sensor board, and connection to a Node.js cloud server, enabling seamless data flow. A brief overview of the project can be seen below hosted on ACM Digital Library.

I overcame a critical issue in the project involving the ESP32's reconnection protocol, which caused system-wide crashes upon WiFi or power interruptions. After extensive testing, debugging, and optimization— including JSON encoding, buffer management, and process pausing—I implemented a stable recovery mechanism using Espressif’s native library functions and system-wide state control, effectively resolving the issue and ensuring uninterrupted streaming and command execution.

I also led the firmware development of the ESP32-CAM module for live video streaming, and helped replace inefficient byte-level header appending with a server-side stream-identification method. I finalized the integration of command handlers for controlling the printer, and synchronized server-issued Epoch timing for precise actuation. Our finalized system streamed synchronized sensor data and live visuals to a secure dashboard, allowing remote monitoring and control of the physical printer in real time. This prototype was featured at SenSys '22 and published in the ACM Digital Library [19].

5G Network KPI Analysis

Conducted an experimental study on the impact of weather conditions on 5G Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) such as latency, signal strength, and throughput. Developed a real-time data collection system using a Raspberry Pi with a 5G hat, integrating environmental data from WeatherAPI. Processed data using Python, Plotly, and Matplotlib, visualizing KPIs with interactive heatmaps to correlate temperature, humidity, and precipitation with network performance.

BlueLink Bluetooth & WiFi IoT Network

Designed and implemented a distributed IoT network using ESP32 microcontrollers connected via Bluetooth and WiFi. The system enables real-time control and automation of:

  • Smart lights with RGB control
  • Smart fans and thermostats
  • Air quality monitoring
  • Flood water detection
Integrated with a user-friendly Android app for seamless smart home automation.
