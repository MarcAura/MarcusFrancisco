EE 553 Reflection: Microfabrication Lab Experience

I entered the class for Microfabrication of Circuits excited to witness the fundamental processes underlying Electrical Engineering. Much of my EE background and undergraduate education is based on transistors, microelectronics, and the core principles of electrical circuit behavior, including properties of current and voltage. From a broad perspective, basic circuits with a DC source, resistors, and reactive components are easy to comprehend. However, delving deeper into Electrical Engineering reveals complexities such as internal resistance and forward biasing. While these phenomena can be analyzed through differential equations and theoretical models, they are difficult to visualize, making direct observation nearly impossible. To witness microfabrication firsthand is to look behind the curtain, replacing conceptual imagination with real-world experiences in the lab. Since learning how a computer worked in elementary school, I had been eager to understand how computer chips, transistors, and memory devices are made. This course provided a pivotal moment where all my undergraduate Electrical Engineering studies converged into an opportunity to observe microfabrication machines in action. In the microfabrication lab, we worked with highly specialized and expensive equipment. We began by learning how to use an ellipsometer, a device that . From there, we explored Electron Beam Lithography (EBL) and had the opportunity to examine an Electron Beam Microscope. These machines range in costand require extensive care, training, and supervision to operate. In the EBL process, we adhered specific metals to silicon by first applying a thin 2um layer of platinum on the spin-coated silicon surface. This platinum layer served as an intermediary to enhance the adhesion of gold, as gold adheres poorly to silicon but bonds well to platinum. One of the most fascinating properties we observed was the way incident light reflects off gold nanoparticles. In EBL, an electron beam is directed at a filament of gold, platinum, or another metal. The beam vaporizes the metal, and the vaporized material is then deposited onto a surface in precise patterns. Using this method, we achieved deposition resolutions as fine as 2um. Gold nanoparticles exhibit size-dependent optical properties, allowing them to reflect different frequencies of light. This phenomenon was historically used in ancient times in stained glass windows, where molten glass mixed with gold nanoparticles produced vibrant red hues. In our lab, we unintentionally observed this effect due to a unique occurrence in the deposition process. The EBL machine uses clips to hold down the sample, which inadvertently blocked some of the vaporized gold deposition. This obstruction created a gradient of gold thickness across the sample. An interesting observation was that this deposition gradient resulted in thicknesses below the minimum deposition capability of the EBL machine. This created a visible color gradient on the sample, which we attributed to the optical properties of gold nanoparticles. Our final report documented all experiments performed throughout the semester, including: , Ellipsometry to measure oxide layer thickness on silicon, Electron Beam Lithography (EBL), E-Beam microscope usage, Microfabrication-grade microscope analysis, Profilometry with a newly acquired profilometer , Hydrofluoric Acid (HF) etching experiments, measuring etch rate over time Additionally, my lab partners and I went beyond the standard course requirements to analyze and document the unexpected gold deposition gradient.

Spin coating was another technique we utilized in our experiments. The HF etching study examined the rate at which silicon was etched per second in the HF solution, providing valuable data on controlled material removal. Getting suited up for the cleanroom was a unique experience. The "bunny suit" worn inside the cleanroom is not for personal safety but rather to protect the sensitive materials and equipment from contamination. Given the extreme sensitivity of these machines, all potential contaminants, including dust and skin particles, must be contained within the suit. Each suit comes in a sterilized plastic package, and we were trained to put them on in a way that prevents external contamination. Before entering the cleanroom, we passed through an air shower, which blasted pressurized air to remove any residual dust or contaminants. My experience in the cleanroom was one of the most valuable aspects of my time at UB. This course provided hands-on exposure to the processes required to fabricate the devices and materials that power modern technology. It reinforced my understanding of Electrical Engineering by grounding theoretical concepts in tangible experiences. Rather than relying solely on diagrams and equations, I now have firsthand knowledge of the fabrication techniques that make microelectronics possible.

This project presented a different challenge. I now need to establish bi-directional intercommunication between two of the same type of device, (ESP32s), but now the devices operate with different connection protocols. My work beforehand was two different types of hardware communicating, but in this project two different types of firmware must communication. One ESP must communicate via WiFi with the internet and the other must communicate via Bluetooth with the WiFi device. The biggest challenge I encountered was programming one of these small, inexpensive devices to use both BLE (Bluetooth Low Energy) and WiFi at the same time. This large challenge concerned the amount of Flash Memory the devices have available. Flash memory on an ESP32 is used to store the program code, libraries, and necessary data for execution, allowing the microcontroller to retain firmware even after a power cycle. When a program exceeds the available flash memory, the device may fail to compile, crash, or enter a reboot loop due to insufficient storage. To accommodate for this, you can optimize memory usage by reducing the program size (removing unused libraries, optimizing code, or using PROGMEM for constants), increasing partition sizes in the ESP32â€™s partition table, or using external flash storage for non-critical data. The device didnâ€™t have enough flash memory to hold the program and execute it properly. The infinite reboot loop is what my ESP32 devices did whenever I attempted to use both communication types as the same time. This gave me a small hint that the ESP had enough memory to store the program but not enough to run it. To fix this, I altered the partition scheme and eventually found a configuration that would support my connection protocol. Other challenges were power usage and optimizing data processing to draw less power and use less memory. The two competing connection protocols caused the chip to draw too much power, causing a "brownout" or low voltage shutdown, and the two types of communication deliver messages in different formats. Command conversion was needed on the chip, and optimizing this was crucial since I had a limited amount of power and space. At one point I realized I was running too many ESP32 devices on one power supply, resulting in a brownout error. This was a separate problem from the brownout shutdown I mentioned earlier. This problem was amongst the easiest to solve since it only required I get another USB power supply, however that didn't prevent the hours of troubleshooting through software before I discovered the real cause of the problem. My approach to problem solving here needed to be consistent and robust if I were to finish this project on time. By this I mean I needed to implement changes iteratively, so most of the time I am able to deduce the source of an error quickly and begin troubleshooting immediately. If I implemented a hundred changes at once, finding issues would be more similar to finding a needle in a haystack. I tackled each problem one at a time, testing multiple devices each time to make sure the problems were not just a faulty chip. Problems arising from manufacturing defects in the chips happened twice in this project. I reallocated flash memory to program storage by changing the partition scheme, implemented power-saving connection methods, and wrote my own command system specifically for this setup. What I am left with is an IoT network with multiple devices. It uses my own connection protocol that I wrote, and since it uses both BLE and WiFi, local operations do not depend on WiFi, so if a user's WiFi goes down, device intercommunication persists. An example of where this would be useful is if you set up routines for a light or device to turn on and off at a certain time, WiFi dependence may become a cumbersome for IoT networks in this application where WiFi is not really even necessary in the first place. This network makes it so WiFi is only used for commands and instructions that need WiFi to communicate with an outside device, leaving the rest up to the Low Power Bluetooth network made up of inexpensive ESP32 devices. Reflection 4: EE 550 - Photovoltaic Technology, Professor Ian R. Sellers

I enrolled in EE 550 expecting to deepen my understanding of photovoltaic (PV) technology beyond the surface-level knowledge I had gained from prior coursework and projects. I had previously worked with solar panels in my senior design project, where I became familiar with key characteristics such as open-circuit voltage, short-circuit current, maximum power point tracking (MPPT), and the equivalent circuit representation of solar cells as a current source with diode behavior. However, this class introduced me to a far more nuanced perspectiveâ€”one that made me re-think how I critically assess emerging technologies and the claims made in research and the industry. One of the most significant takeaways from this course was learning to approach new photovoltaic technologies with a more critical mindset. In 2021, I wrote a paper on perovskite solar cells, a technology widely hailed for its potential to revolutionize the solar industry due to its high efficiency, tunable bandgap, and low-cost fabrication. At the time, my research was based on recent publications that portrayed perovskites as the next major leap in solar power. While I acknowledged concerns regarding their stability and service life, I largely focused on their advantages and future prospects. However, in EE 550, I gained a more balanced and skeptical perspective on the practical feasibility of perovskite-based photovoltaics. Through discussions with Professor Sellers during class, I learned that much of the excitement surrounding perovskites is fueled by highly selective data, with many research papers presenting only the most optimistic results while glossing over fundamental reliability issues. For example, while perovskite solar cells can demonstrate efficiencies exceeding 25\% in controlled lab environments, their long-term stability is a major concern. Many perovskite cells degrade within weeks, far from the decades-long service life expected from commercial solar panels. Additionally, yield reporting in perovskite manufacturing is often absent, meaning that while individual cells may be inexpensive to produce, the true cost skyrockets if hundreds must be fabricated to obtain a single functional unit. This realization reshaped my view of technological hype in renewable energy. While innovations are necessary to advance solar power, the premature excitement surrounding some emerging technologies can lead to unrealistic expectations and, ultimately, public distrust when those technologies fail to deliver on exaggerated promises. It is crucial to distinguish between lab-scale success and real-world viability, considering not only efficiency but also manufacturing scalability, stability, and deployment challenges. I wrote a paper on Perovskite usage in solar panels in 2021. My report adresses some of the issues mentioned here, but it glances over the more concerning fact that perovskites are not proven to have a suitable service life for Photovoltaic panels. My report written on perovskites can be accessed via this link: Perovskite Solar Cells Report What I found in this class was a more realistic view on how useful perovskites are for widespread use. Professor Sellers was able to look past the hype, and was not easily convinced of perovskite panel feasibility like I was in years prior. Beyond perovskites, this course also provided valuable insight into the engineering and design principles behind PV systems. One particularly interesting topic was the role of boost converters in photovoltaic applications. As I am simultaneously studying power electronics in another course, I found it fascinating to see the direct application of DC-DC converters in maximizing the efficiency of solar energy conversion. Boost converters are commonly used in PV systems to step up the voltage from solar panels while maintaining maximum power transfer through MPPT algorithms. This connection between power electronics and photovoltaics reinforced how interdisciplinary renewable energy systems truly are. Another key takeaway was the complexity of integrating solar power into the electrical grid. Many people assume that the main challenge with solar power is simply storing excess energy to compensate for intermittency. While energy storage is important, EE 550 introduced me to the numerous technical and economic challenges beyond just storage. These include grid stability, voltage regulation, power electronics for grid interconnection, and the impact of variable solar generation on traditional power infrastructure. Overall, EE 550 has significantly refined my critical thinking when evaluating solar technologies. It has taught me to question the sustainability and practicality of emerging PV solutions rather than accepting optimistic projections at face value. While I still believe that perovskites and other next-generation materials may play a role in the future of solar energy, I now understand the importance of long-term reliability, economic feasibility, and scalable manufacturing as key factors in determining whether a new technology will succeed beyond the laboratory. This course has not only strengthened my technical understanding of photovoltaics but also provided me with a more grounded perspectiveâ€”one that will be invaluable in my career as an engineer working in renewable energy. Moving forward, I will continue to approach emerging technologies with a balance of curiosity and skepticism, ensuring that my assessments are based on both scientific rigor and real-world viability.

Introduction to Tapecon and its FHE Evolution

Tapecon is a company that has produced tape, stickers, adhesives, prints, and various other products throughout most of its history. Recently, Tapecon has begun leveraging its expertise in adhesives and printing to produce Flexible Hybrid Electronics (FHE)â€”a new category of electronics that integrates printed circuits on flexible dielectric sheets rather than using traditional rigid circuit boards. This approach allows for thin, compact, and flexible electronics that have applications in wearable technology, medical devices, and industrial automation.

Designing Printed Circuitry for FHE

The FHE design process begins with high-resolution film printing, followed by UV exposure to create a negative of the circuit design. A fine stainless-steel mesh coated in UV-sensitive resin is used to apply conductive ink, which seeps through the unexposed areas, forming the conductive traces on PET plastic or paper substrates.

The resulting conductive patterns allow for surface-mount component placement, enabling the creation of fully functional electronic circuits without the bulky form factor of traditional PCBs.

Challenges and Problem-Solving

One of the main challenges in FHE development is achieving sustainability while maintaining efficiency. Traditional PCBs use large amounts of metal and non-recyclable materials, whereas FHE circuits can be printed with small amounts of conductive inks on flexible substrates, reducing waste.

One of my first projects involved designing an FHE flood detector circuit using only a BJT transistor and conductive traces. The industry standard for flood detection circuits requires a 3-inch circuit board, but I was able to reduce the footprint to less than a half-inch while maintaining full functionality.

Technical Achievements and Contributions

My work at Tapecon extended beyond just designing FHE circuits. I also developed programming and debugging devices used for testing FHE circuits before deployment. One of my key contributions was the FHE temperature detector circuit, which was showcased at the NextFlex Conference in California and Berlin in 2023.

  • Developed a temperature-sensing FHE circuit using an AtTiny24A microcontroller.
  • Designed a paper-thin sensor that illuminates an LED when temperature thresholds are exceeded.
  • Demonstrated potential applications in cold-chain logistics and medical wearables.
