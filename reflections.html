Neural networks operate in a way that more closely resembles the human brain than traditional circuits. Seen above is a basic demonstration of how a neural network works. Adjusting these weights based on training data allows a neural network to learn patterns and make predictions on future data. A common application of neural networks is handwriting recognition. I took EE 553 to gain hands-on experience with the microfabrication processes that power modern electronics. From ellipsometry to electron beam lithography, each lab session deepened my appreciation for the precision and complexity behind semiconductor manufacturing.

I entered the class for Microfabrication of Circuits excited to witness the fundamental processes underlying Electrical Engineering. Much of my EE background and undergraduate education is based on transistors, microelectronics, and the core principles of electrical circuit behavior, including properties of current and voltage. From a broad perspective, basic circuits with a DC source, resistors, and reactive components are easy to comprehend. However, delving deeper into Electrical Engineering reveals complexities such as internal resistance and forward biasing. While these phenomena can be analyzed through differential equations and theoretical models, they are difficult to visualize, making direct observation nearly impossible.

To witness microfabrication firsthand is to look behind the curtain, replacing conceptual imagination with real-world experiences in the lab. Since learning how a computer worked in elementary school, I had been eager to understand how computer chips, transistors, and memory devices are made. This course provided a pivotal moment where all my undergraduate Electrical Engineering studies converged into an opportunity to observe microfabrication machines in action.

In the microfabrication lab, we worked with highly specialized and expensive equipment. We began by learning how to use an ellipsometer, a device that measures the thickness and optical properties of thin films by analyzing changes in the polarization of reflected light, allowing us to characterize deposited materials with nanometer precision.

From there, we explored Electron Beam Lithography (EBL) and underwent the entire process of using that machine. During my time in the cleanroom I also got to see an electron microscope. These machines range in cost, and require extensive care, training, and supervision to operate. To quote the University of Houston Nanofabrication Facility, "An entry level EBL system costs approximately $1,000,000 and more expensive systems are engineered to write faster and over larger areas." [9] In the EBL process, we adhered specific metals to silicon by first applying a thin 2um layer of platinum on the spin-coated silicon surface. This platinum layer served as an intermediary to enhance the adhesion of gold, as gold adheres poorly to silicon but bonds well to platinum.

One of the most fascinating properties we observed was the way incident light reflects off gold nanoparticles. In EBL, an electron beam is directed at a filament of gold, platinum, or another metal. The beam vaporizes the metal, and the vaporized material is then deposited onto a surface in precise patterns. Using this method, we achieved deposition resolutions as fine as 2um. One of our test samples deposited the names of our lab members on a silicon wafer. This sample can be seen in the figure below shown under a microscope. The names are present as a thin layer of gold, and the font is on a nanometer scale.

Gold nanoparticles exhibit size-dependent optical properties, allowing them to reflect different frequencies of light. This phenomenon was historically used in ancient times in stained glass windows, where molten glass mixed with gold nanoparticles produced vibrant red hues. In our lab, we unintentionally observed this effect due to a unique occurrence in the deposition process. The EBL machine uses clips to hold down the sample, which inadvertently blocked some of the vaporized gold deposition. This obstruction created a gradient of gold thickness across the sample.

An interesting observation was that this deposition gradient resulted in thicknesses below the minimum deposition capability of the EBL machine. This created a visible color gradient on the sample, which we attributed to the optical properties of gold nanoparticles. We had initially assumed that the color gradient was an effect of the microscope, however upon recalling the properties of gold nanoparticles and the effect particle size has on light, I piped up and explained to our group that it must be a deposition thickness gradient. While outside the scope of the class, me and my lab partners wrote about this effect in detail in our final report. Having prior knowledge of the effect of gold nanoparticles, I contributed much of the background information to this report.

Aside from the gold nanoparticle experiment, the final report documented all experiments performed throughout the semester, including: Ellipsometry to measure oxide layer thickness on silicon, Electron Beam Lithography (EBL), Profilometry with a newly acquired profilometer, and Hydrofluoric Acid (HF) etching experiments measuring etch rate over time.

Spin coating was another technique we utilized in our experiments. Spin coating is a technique used in microfabrication to apply uniform thin films by depositing a liquid solution onto a substrate and rapidly spinning it. The centrifugal force spreads the liquid evenly, and as the solvent evaporates, a thin, uniform coating remains, with thickness controlled by spin speed, viscosity, and duration.

The HF etching study examined the rate at which silicon was etched per second in the HF solution, providing valuable data on controlled material removal.

Getting suited up for the cleanroom was a unique experience. The "bunny suit" worn inside the cleanroom is not for personal safety but rather to protect the sensitive materials and equipment from contamination. Given the extreme sensitivity of these machines, all potential contaminants, including dust and skin particles, must be contained within the suit. Each suit comes in a sterilized plastic package, and we were trained to put them on in a way that prevents external contamination. Before entering the cleanroom, we passed through an air shower, which blasted pressurized air to remove any residual dust or contaminants.

My experience in the cleanroom was one of the most valuable aspects of my time at UB. This course provided hands-on exposure to the processes required to fabricate the devices and materials that power modern technology. It reinforced my understanding of Electrical Engineering by grounding theoretical concepts in tangible experiences. Rather than relying solely on diagrams and equations, I now have firsthand knowledge of the fabrication techniques that make microelectronics possible.

The biggest challenge I encountered was programming one of these small, inexpensive devices to use both BLE (Bluetooth Low Energy) and WiFi at the same time. This large challenge concerned the amount of Flash Memory the devices have available. Flash memory on an ESP32 is used to store the program code, libraries, and necessary data for execution, allowing the microcontroller to retain firmware even after a power cycle. When a program exceeds the available flash memory, the device may fail to compile, crash, or enter a reboot loop due to insufficient storage. To accommodate for this, you can optimize memory usage by reducing the program size (removing unused libraries, optimizing code, or using PROGMEM for constants), increasing partition sizes in the ESP32â€™s partition table, or using external flash storage for non-critical data.

The device didnâ€™t have enough flash memory to hold the program and execute it properly. The infinite reboot loop is what my ESP32 devices did whenever I attempted to use both communication types as the same time. This gave me a small hint that the ESP had enough memory to store the program but not enough to run it. To fix this, I altered the partition scheme and eventually found a configuration that would support my connection protocol.

Other challenges were power usage and optimizing data processing to draw less power and use less memory. The two competing connection protocols caused the chip to draw too much power, causing a "brownout" or low voltage shutdown, and the two types of communication deliver messages in different formats. Command conversion was needed on the chip, and optimizing this was crucial since I had a limited amount of power and space. At one point I realized I was running too many ESP32 devices on one power supply, resulting in a brownout error. This was a separate problem from the brownout shutdown I mentioned earlier. This problem was amongst the easiest to solve since it only required I get another USB power supply, however that didn't prevent the hours of troubleshooting through software before I discovered the real cause of the problem.

My approach to problem solving here needed to be consistent and robust if I were to finish this project on time. By this I mean I needed to implement changes iteratively, so most of the time I am able to deduce the source of an error quickly and begin troubleshooting immediately. If I implemented a hundred changes at once, finding issues would be more similar to finding a needle in a haystack. I tackled each problem one at a time, testing multiple devices each time to make sure the problems were not just a faulty chip. Problems arising from manufacturing defects in the chips happened twice in this project. I reallocated flash memory to program storage by changing the partition scheme, implemented power-saving connection methods, and wrote my own command system specifically for this setup.

What I am left with is an IoT network with multiple devices. It uses my own connection protocol that I wrote, and since it uses both BLE and WiFi, local operations do not depend on WiFi, so if a user's WiFi goes down, device intercommunication persists. An example of where this would be useful is if you set up routines for a light or device to turn on and off at a certain time, WiFi dependence may become a cumbersome for IoT networks in this application where WiFi is not really even necessary in the first place. This network makes it so WiFi is only used for commands and instructions that need WiFi to communicate with an outside device, leaving the rest up to the Low Power Bluetooth network made up of inexpensive ESP32 devices.

I enrolled in EE 550 expecting to deepen my understanding of photovoltaic (PV) technology beyond the surface-level knowledge I had gained from prior coursework and projects. I had previously worked with solar panels in my senior design project, where I became familiar with key characteristics such as open-circuit voltage, short-circuit current, maximum power point tracking (MPPT), and the equivalent circuit representation of solar cells as a current source with diode behavior. However, this class introduced me to a far more nuanced perspectiveâ€”one that made me re-think how I critically assess emerging technologies and the claims made in research and the industry.

One of the most significant takeaways from this course was learning to approach new photovoltaic technologies with a more critical mindset. In 2021, I wrote a paper on perovskite solar cells, a technology widely hailed for its potential to revolutionize the solar industry due to its high efficiency, tunable bandgap, and low-cost fabrication. At the time, my research was based on recent publications that portrayed perovskites as the next major leap in solar power. While I acknowledged concerns regarding their stability and service life, I largely focused on their advantages and future prospects. However, in EE 550, I gained a more balanced and skeptical perspective on the practical feasibility of perovskite-based photovoltaics.

Through discussions with Professor Sellers during class, I learned that much of the excitement surrounding perovskites is fueled by highly selective data, with many research papers presenting only the most optimistic results while glossing over fundamental reliability issues. For example, while perovskite solar cells can demonstrate efficiencies exceeding 25% in controlled lab environments, their long-term stability is a major concern. Many perovskite cells degrade within weeks, far from the decades-long service life expected from commercial solar panels. Additionally, yield reporting in perovskite manufacturing is often absent, meaning that while individual cells may be inexpensive to produce, the true cost skyrockets if hundreds must be fabricated to obtain a single functional unit.

This realization reshaped my view of technological hype in renewable energy. While innovations are necessary to advance solar power, the premature excitement surrounding some emerging technologies can lead to unrealistic expectations and, ultimately, public distrust when those technologies fail to deliver on exaggerated promises. It is crucial to distinguish between lab-scale success and real-world viability, considering not only efficiency but also manufacturing scalability, stability, and deployment challenges.

I wrote a paper on Perovskite usage in solar panels in 2021. My report addresses some of the issues mentioned here, but it glances over the more concerning fact that perovskites are not proven to have a suitable service life for Photovoltaic panels. My report written on perovskites overlooked many of the shortcomings and focused mainly on the potential to improve cell efficiency. What I found in this class was a more realistic view on how useful perovskites are for widespread use. Professor Sellers was able to look past the hype, and was not easily convinced of perovskite panel feasibility like I was in years prior.

Beyond perovskites, this course also provided valuable insight into the engineering and design principles behind PV systems. One particularly interesting topic was the role of boost converters in photovoltaic applications. As I am simultaneously studying power electronics in another course, I found it fascinating to see the direct application of DC-DC converters in maximizing the efficiency of solar energy conversion. Boost converters are commonly used in PV systems to step up the voltage from solar panels while maintaining maximum power transfer through MPPT algorithms. This connection between power electronics and photovoltaics reinforced how interdisciplinary renewable energy systems truly are.

Another key takeaway was the complexity of integrating solar power into the electrical grid. Many people assume that the main challenge with solar power is simply storing excess energy to compensate for intermittency. While energy storage is important, EE 550 introduced me to the numerous technical and economic challenges beyond just storage. These include grid stability, voltage regulation, power electronics for grid interconnection, and the impact of variable solar generation on traditional power infrastructure.

Overall, EE 550 has significantly refined my critical thinking when evaluating solar technologies. It has taught me to question the sustainability and practicality of emerging PV solutions rather than accepting optimistic projections at face value. While I still believe that perovskites and other next-generation materials may play a role in the future of solar energy, I now understand the importance of long-term reliability, economic feasibility, and scalable manufacturing as key factors in determining whether a new technology will succeed beyond the laboratory.

This course has not only strengthened my technical understanding of photovoltaics but also provided me with a more grounded perspectiveâ€”one that will be invaluable in my career as an engineer working in renewable energy. Moving forward, I will continue to approach emerging technologies with a balance of curiosity and skepticism, ensuring that my assessments are based on both scientific rigor and real-world viability.

Decision analysis that extends the domain of decision-making problems from those considered in traditional statistical hypothesis testing scenarios: modeling decisions, which emphasize structuring decision problems using techniques such as influence diagrams and decision trees; modeling uncertainty, which covers subjective probability assessment, use of classical probability models, Bayesian analysis, and value of information; and modeling preferences, which introduces concepts of risk preference, expected utility, and multi-attribute value and utility models. This course is dual-listed with IE 512.

My Decision Analysis Project

My project is relevant to me for a personal reason. In Fall of 2024 I was told by my doctor that I had many of the warning signs for thyroid cancer. I had abnormal blood thyroglobulin levels, and was informed by an ENT that I was a 4/5 on the TiRADS thyroid cancer risk assessment scale. I was unsure of what all the statistics meant at the time and was understandably panicked at the now very real possibility of going through my last year of college as a cancer patient.

Through research and consultation with many doctors, I was able to more adequately understand the numbers and the diagnosis. Thus, I decided to make my final project a decision tree, sensitivity analysis, and decision analysis of what actions to take when diagnosed with a thyroid nodule.

Decision Tree

The decision tree models the critical choices faced by patients with thyroid nodules, beginning with the presence or absence of a nodule and branching into treatment paths such as active monitoring or thyroidectomy. It integrates statistical probabilities and patient utilitiesâ€”like quality of life and longevityâ€”based on nodule size, malignancy rates, and mental well-being. Each branch reflects real-world outcomes, revealing that early specialist consultation often leads to the highest expected value. It also models how different diagnostic results influence decision-making clarity and survival likelihood [1].

This subject ended up being a great choice of project material, since I had already done extensive research on thyroid cancer, and was visiting thyroid surgeons and specialists to acquire the most accurate information I could. My goal was to be the most informed on the condition and on my choices as I possibly could, and I was invested in making this project useful as I was personally going through this difficult choice during that semester.

The influence diagram presents a systems-level overview of the decision-making process by mapping interactions between decisions, uncertainties, and outcomes. Nodes represent elements such as symptoms, expert opinion, actual malignancy, hormone therapy, and quality of lifeâ€”each with directional influences. Notably, a bidirectional arrow connects expert opinion and actual malignancy, acknowledging uncertainty in diagnosis. The model emphasizes that while surgery yields a definitive diagnosis, it comes with risks like complications and lifelong hormone replacement. This visual highlights the delicate tradeoffs between survival, diagnosis confidence, and patient mental well-being [1].

In our project, we modeled utility in terms of both life expectancy and psychological impact. Our double-objective framework allowed us to weight not just survival time, but also mental burden. We learned that longevity is the dominant utility driver, but mental health has meaningful weightâ€”especially in cases with inconclusive test results.

One surprising insight was that in many cases, waiting and monitoring was statistically the optimal strategy, especially for small or indeterminate nodules, which have malignancy risks between 10.5%-15% depending on size [14]. Our influence diagram also captured feedback loops from expert opinions and test results.

My Experience with a Thyroid Condition

The most central challenge to this problem is that doctors cannot diagnose thyroid cancer with certainty without removing the organ. There are scans, blood tests, MRIs, CT scans, Ultrasound, needle aspiration biopsies, and physical exams that can give us clues, but none tell us for certain weather or not you have cancer. I underwent each one of these tests, and at the time I was holding out hope that one of them would tell me for sure that I donâ€™t have cancer, but that did not happen. Scans like the MRI and CT were done with and without contrast, making them time consuming. Interestingly, these scans were taken of my thyroid (neck) and my sinuses and maxilofacial region in one go. For that reason, I my mouth was stuffed with gauze to maintain contrast between the soft palate and the tongue during the duration of the scan, where I was required to keep completely still for 50 minutes.

I soon came to understand what TiRADS means, a radiologic scoring system used to assess the risk of thyroid nodule malignancy, and I understood the nuance of what thyroglobulin levels indicate. Through my journey making decisions about this condition for myself, I discovered more of the mistakes one may make along the way. One of the pitfalls I found is going into surgery to remove the thyroid prematurely. A thyroidectomy leads to the need for lifelong hormone replacement therapy, even when no cancer is present. In fact, 56% of surgeries for indeterminate nodules in certain populations turn out to have been unnecessary [18].

So.. Should I Remove the Organ?

Indeterminant biopsy results face you with a tough decision. I had an inconclusive biopsy, which means the choice to go into surgery is ultimately up to me. Without all the information, you need to consider what statistics are available, and make a decision based on the limited information and the likely incidence of cancer for your set of symptoms. I faced the difficult reality of choosing between living with the possibility of cancer and undergoing a permanent, potentially unnecessary surgery.

The Choice

The trouble is that going into surgery means removing the thyroid, which means you will need to undergo hormone replacement therapy for the rest of your life. If you remove the thyroid suspecting cancer, but after the fact discover that no cancer was present, you've removed an important organ from your body permanently and it was unnecessary. The feeling of discovering you just underwent an unnecessary life altering surgery is difficult to describe for patients, but I have heard it described as a mixture of happiness and regret. You're pleased because now you know for certain that you don't have cancer, but you lament the fact that you did not need to remove the thyroid and now need hormone replacement therapy.

This choice has implications beyond the simple cancer diagnosis. If you keep getting indeterminant biopsies like I did my first three visits you are forced to live with the idea that you must choose the right option. You need to walk around every day with the knowledge that you may have cancer and are choosing not to have it removed. It is impossible to say if this alternate possibility is better than hormone replacement, but the psychological impact of this decision is quite significant. My case was not severe by most metrics, but still my mental health suffered with the weight of this decision on my mind. I was unable to focus in class, and was constantly researching thyroid cancer, trying to figure out my rate of survival given each course of action.

Consulting a Specialist

I cannot emphasize enough that consulting with a thyroid surgeon was an absolute must. He gave me the information and confidence to form a decision. I was given a through explanation of the condition and all the deciding factors, a conversation that lasted over an hour. I was given the choice to get another biopsy or to monitor with ultrasound. I even suggested draining the nodule to reduce cystic content for better biopsy sampling.

Alternatives to Surgery

I knew that statistically speaking the it is very unlikely that next biopsy will provide useful results after several non-diagnostic tests. I asked about alternative biopsies such as surgical or core-needle biopsies. These both have their own set of severe drawbacks. A surgical biopsy is considered so dangerous it is no longer permitted in the medical field, due to the likelihood that the lyrangeal nerve will be damaged or severed. A core needle biopsy uses a larger needle, introducing more risks, longer recovery, and a risk to damage the carotid artery.

I eventually proposed the idea of draining fluid during a traditional fine needle aspiration biopsy. This would make the nodule behave less cystic (fluid filled) and possibly make the tissue easier to collect inside the needle. I was nervous to ask the interventional radiologist to try something different that I hadn't even heard of online, but I was determined to find a different strategy to improve my chances. I am pleased to report that my latest biopsy came back with more conclusive results. It was characterized and is associated with a far lower risk of cancer than the odds I was given back in September of 2024. I reflect on reading these results and consider how close I was to choosing surgery. In November of 2024 I was certain that my appointment with the surgeon would result in booking a surgery. By January of 2025 I had a more concrete understanding of the condition and made the right decision regarding surgery vs active monitoring.

Ultimately, this project taught me how to bring clarity to a deeply ambiguous real-world challenge. It wasn't just an exercise in numbers or decision theoryâ€”it gave me language and structure to think through the most important decision of my life to date. I now understand what it means to weigh tradeoffs under uncertainty, how to identify meaningful objectives, and how to respect the influence of both quantitative and qualitative outcomes. This course helped me become not just a better decision-maker, but a more analytical and self-aware individual during a difficult time.

Course Description

EE 550LEC - Digital Integrated Circuits

This graduate-level course explores the design, analysis, and optimization of CMOS digital circuits, emphasizing both theoretical principles and practical implementation. Topics include MOSFET device physics, combinational and sequential logic design, datapath subsystems, and memory architectures such as SRAM and DRAM. Students gain hands-on experience using industry-standard EDA tools like Cadence for schematic capture, simulation, and layout, as well as Verilog HDL for hardware description and synthesis using Vivado. The course culminates in a design project requiring students to implement and evaluate a complex digital system. Through lectures, labs, and simulation assignments, students develop skills in logical effort, gate sizing, performance analysis, and real-world digital circuit challenges.

At the heart of digital circuit design lies the careful manipulation of transistor-level structures within integrated circuits. Understanding the electrical characteristics of devices like BJTs and MOSFETs is essential to predicting their behavior and optimizing circuit performance. Concepts such as IV curves, threshold voltages, and parasitic effects are foundational in modeling how these devices will operate under different conditions.

In the case of BJTs and other traditional transistors, designers must understand the relationship between current and voltage in forward-active operation, cutoff, and saturation regions. These principles are captured in characteristic IV curves, which help define expected performance across input and output conditions.

As designs scale down and shift toward MOSFET-based logic, additional factors come into play. MOSFET layouts must account not only for electrical properties, but also for physical constraints such as chip area, signal delay, and fabrication cost. In high-density designs, parasitic capacitance between terminals can impact switching performance, and routing complexity increases with the number of transistors.

To address these challenges, designers often employ techniques such as overlapping doped silicon regions to reduce overall area and interconnect length. Efficient physical layout directly contributes to performance, power efficiency, and scalability in advanced digital systems.

Introduction to Tapecon and its FHE Evolution

Tapecon is a company that has produced tape, stickers, adhesives, prints, and various other products throughout most of its history. Recently, Tapecon has begun leveraging its expertise in adhesives and printing to produce Flexible Hybrid Electronics (FHE)â€”a new category of electronics that integrates printed circuits on flexible dielectric sheets rather than using traditional rigid circuit boards. This approach allows for thin, compact, and flexible electronics that have applications in wearable technology, medical devices, and industrial automation.

Challenges and Problem-Solving

One of the main challenges in FHE development is achieving sustainability while maintaining efficiency. Traditional PCBs use large amounts of metal and non-recyclable materials, whereas FHE circuits can be printed with small amounts of conductive inks on flexible substrates, reducing waste.

One of my first projects involved designing an FHE flood detector circuit using only a BJT transistor and conductive traces. The industry standard for flood detection circuits requires a 3-inch circuit board, but I was able to reduce the footprint to less than a half-inch while maintaining full functionality.

Technical Achievements and Contributions

My work at Tapecon extended beyond just designing FHE circuits. I also developed programming and debugging devices used for testing FHE circuits before deployment. One of my key contributions was the FHE temperature detector circuit, which was showcased at the NextFlex Conference in California and Berlin in 2023.

  • Developed a temperature-sensing FHE circuit using an AtTiny24A microcontroller.
  • Designed a paper-thin sensor that illuminates an LED when temperature thresholds are exceeded.
  • Demonstrated potential applications in cold-chain logistics and medical wearables.

My co-op experience at Tapecon has been incredibly rewarding. Over the past two semesters and two summers, I have had the opportunity to work with cutting-edge technology, refine my engineering skills, and contribute to projects that were presented at international conferences.
